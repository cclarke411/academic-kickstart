LAVAR is the most substantial product thread represented in my OpenAI work archive. Across planning, grant development, product documentation, and presentation work, I used LAVAR as the anchor for a broader effort to design an AI-supported learning platform that combines voice interaction, explanation-centered learning, adaptive questioning, and accessible user experiences.

My work on this project covered both product definition and communication. The exported OpenAI conversation history includes project-overview work, grant abstracts, summit slide material, K-12 RFP responses, fellowship budgeting, summary documentation, pitch scripting, and formative assessment design. That mix suggests a hands-on role spanning concept development, educational positioning, narrative design, and operational planning rather than a single isolated deliverable.

At the product level, I framed LAVAR as more than a conventional chatbot. The recurring themes point to a system designed to support learning through spoken reasoning, explanation, revision, and feedback. I explored how voice and conversational AI could be used not only for content delivery, but also for deeper instructional functions such as prompting reflection, diagnosing misconceptions, and supporting learners who benefit from lower-friction, more accessible interaction patterns.

I also translated the platform into multiple external-facing formats. The archive shows work on grant-writing material, fellowship budgets, RFP responses, pitch scripts, and summit decks, which means I was actively turning the product into language that funders, partners, and educational stakeholders could understand and support. That required moving between technical framing, educational outcomes, accessibility rationale, and venture-style storytelling depending on the audience.

One especially important part of the project was the development of **formative dialogue assessments** for LAVAR. In that work, I described assessment as a structured conversation in which a learner explains, justifies, revises, and reflects rather than simply selecting a correct answer. I positioned LAVAR as a system that could capture reasoning quality, confidence, misconceptions, and improvement over time, then turn those signals into actionable feedback for both learners and educators.

Overall, this project represents my ability to connect product strategy, AI interaction design, learning science, grant development, and communication. LAVAR sits at the intersection of accessibility, voice interfaces, and education technology, and the materials in the archive show sustained work to shape it as a credible product, research, and funding opportunity.
